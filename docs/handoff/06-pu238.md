# Stage 6 — Wire up Pu-238

*Read `00-OVERVIEW.md` first. Stages 1–5 must be confirmed complete.*

Pu-238 reuses the cube-mode rendering pipeline from Stage 4 — same Shiba, same Y-axis, same viewport-scaling logic, same CSS-mm cube edge derived from `cbrt(volumeCm3) * 10`. Pure Pu-238 metal at **19.8 g/cm³** density (fractionally denser than gold). The visualisation has the luxury of nothing spontaneously disassembling — pure plutonium metal at multi-kilogram mass would self-destruct from its own decay heat in reality, but the cube on screen is what the dial says it is. The "would melt itself in reality" caption appears at relevant thresholds as honest commentary on what physics says about the real world.

The differences from gold/silver are well-defined: cube material visual, **two-channel glow overlay** (intensity and colour temperature scaling independently per the blackbody emission ladder from Stage 3), **opt-in Geiger crackle** synthesising real Poisson click events, brand-voice clarification caption, and substitution of `<Pu238FactCard />` for `<QuantityAnchorCard />`.

## What to build

### Pu-238 panel structure

1. **Brand-voice clarification (above the panel).** Render persistently: *"Plutonium-238 — the radioisotope that powers spacecraft. Non-fissile, not weapons material."* Style: small, slightly muted, but always present and never hidden. This is mandatory — a one-line clarification near the readout, in the same spirit as the uranium fuel pellet's eventual LEU-not-HEU clarification.

2. **Cube + Shiba + Y-axis** — exactly as Stage 4, with:
   - `densityGramsPerCm3 = 19.8`
   - Sprite path → Pu-238 metal cube asset (matte metallic surface, slight oxidation patina if the Blender pipeline supports it)
   - `cbrt(volumeCm3) * 10` for cube edge in mm
   - Shiba at true 40 cm height alongside, viewport-scaled
   - `<YAxis />` to the left of the cube

3. **`<CubeGlowOverlay massGrams={currentMassGrams} />`** — overlays the cube and the area immediately around it. Glow scales continuously per the two-channel logic from Stage 3:
   - `massToColorTemp()` → hue position along the blackbody emission ladder
   - `massToIntensity()` → brightness, opacity, bloom radius
   - Both interpolated smoothly via CSS custom properties; intensity climbs faster than colour temperature
   - Colour stops anchored to actual incandescent emission temperatures (~600 °C dull red → ~1500 °C white-hot)
   - Warning caption fires at mass ≥ 1 kg

4. **`<GeigerCrackle massGrams={currentMassGrams} audioEnabled={audioState} />`** (audio toggle component below).

5. **Audio toggle button.** A small speaker icon in the panel header, local to the Pu-238 panel — *not* in the global header. Reads/writes `?audio=on` / `?audio=off` URL state per Stage 2's URL state additions. Default off. Visual states: muted (default, slash through speaker), enabled (clean speaker), and a tiny "synthesised" tooltip on hover so the user knows it's not a sample loop.

6. **Readout strip below the cube**, in this order:

   **a. Primary mass readout (large bold).** oz primary, g secondary. When `warningCaption` from `<CubeGlowOverlay />` is true, append " *(would melt itself in reality)*" to the mass line.

   **b. Volume + cube edge** — secondary lines, same format as gold/silver.

   **c. Material cost line.** "$X material cost · 1 BTC buys Y g." Driven by the **material cost ($5,000/g)** — that's the canonical equivalence. Fully-loaded program cost ($100,000/g) is referenced only on the methodology page, not on the panel.

   **d. Activity readout (small, persistent).** "Activity: X Ci · Y disintegrations per second." Driven by `specificActivityCiPerGram = 17` from the schema. Sets up the user's intuition for what they're hearing if they enable audio.

   **e. `<Pu238FactCard currentMassGrams={...} />`** — slider-position-keyed contextual fact, occupying the same slot the gold/silver panels use for `<QuantityAnchorCard />`.

   **f. Source attribution row (small persistent).** *"DOE Office of Nuclear Energy · NASA Planetary Science · The Planetary Society · Cassini OIG (1997, escalated) · See methodology."*

7. **No quantity anchors.** Pu-238 doesn't use `<QuantityAnchorCard />` — its editorial layer is `<Pu238FactCard />` instead, which is keyed to slider position rather than proximity-fired against a sparse mass landscape.

## `<GeigerCrackle />` — full spec

Pu-238-specific. Built and wired in this stage rather than abstracted to Stage 3, because its lifecycle (audio context, intersection observer, URL state coupling) is non-trivial and the abstraction would just be bookkeeping.

### Behaviour

- **Default off.** Never autoplays. Audio context is created lazily on first user interaction (the toggle button click) — required by all modern browsers.
- **Click events are synthesised, not sampled.** Each click is a band-limited noise burst (~3–5 ms envelope, centred ~4 kHz with ±500 Hz randomisation per click) so it doesn't sound mechanical or canned. ~50 lines of Web Audio code; no audio asset to author or licence.
- **Click rate is Poisson-distributed.** Real Geiger output is stochastic — random clicks at a rate proportional to activity. Looping a fixed pattern sounds wrong. Schedule next click at `now + (-Math.log(Math.random()) / rate)` seconds, recurring.
- **Rate is linear in mass.** Pu-238 specific activity is ~17 Ci per gram. So `clicksPerSecond = massGrams * 17 * SAMPLE_RATIO`, where `SAMPLE_RATIO` is a tuning constant chosen so the audio is musical rather than seizure-inducing. Suggested calibration:
  - At 1 g → ~3 clicks/sec (sparse, distinct, recognisable)
  - At 100 g → ~30 clicks/sec (clear counter pattern)
  - At 10 kg → ~300 clicks/sec (clicks merge perceptually into hiss)
  - At market cap → continuous hiss, capped at ~1000/sec for performance
- **Cap at 1000 clicks/sec.** Above this, schedule overhead dominates and the perceptual difference is nil. Cap at 1000/sec; let everything above market-cap-equivalent quantities just sit there hissing.
- **Per-click volume stays roughly constant.** What changes is rate. As rate climbs, clicks blend perceptually — exactly the cue real Geiger counters give for "you should leave the room."
- **Stops cleanly when:**
  - The audio toggle is flipped off
  - The Pu-238 panel scrolls out of viewport (use `IntersectionObserver`)
  - The user navigates away from the page
  - The slider mass drops below 1 g (no clicks at imperceptible activity)

### Accessibility

- **Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`.** Some users set this when sensitive to overstimulation broadly; treat as a proxy for audio sensitivity. When set, audio toggle defaults to disabled and shows a small "(reduced-motion preference detected)" hint on the toggle.
- **Visual click indicator alongside the audio.** A small dot near the speaker icon that pulses on each click event. Deaf and hard-of-hearing users get the same information channel; sighted users with audio off see the click pattern visualised. The pulse is ~80 ms fade-out per click so it stays distinct at low rates and blurs into a steady glow at high rates — same perceptual logic as the audio.

### Implementation outline

```typescript
class GeigerEngine {
  private audioContext: AudioContext | null = null;
  private nextClickTimeoutId: number | null = null;
  private currentRate: number = 0;
  private enabled: boolean = false;
  private inViewport: boolean = false;

  setMass(massGrams: number): void {
    const SAMPLE_RATIO = 0.18; // tuning constant
    this.currentRate = Math.min(1000, massGrams * 17 * SAMPLE_RATIO);
    if (this.currentRate < 1) this.stop();
  }

  setEnabled(enabled: boolean): void {
    this.enabled = enabled;
    if (enabled && this.inViewport && this.currentRate >= 1) this.scheduleNext();
    else this.stop();
  }

  setInViewport(inViewport: boolean): void {
    this.inViewport = inViewport;
    if (this.enabled) this.setEnabled(this.enabled); // re-evaluate
  }

  private scheduleNext(): void {
    if (!this.enabled || !this.inViewport || this.currentRate < 1) return;
    const intervalMs = (-Math.log(Math.random()) / this.currentRate) * 1000;
    this.nextClickTimeoutId = setTimeout(() => {
      this.emitClick();
      this.scheduleNext();
    }, intervalMs);
  }

  private emitClick(): void {
    // synthesise band-limited noise burst, dispatch CustomEvent for visual indicator
  }

  private stop(): void {
    if (this.nextClickTimeoutId !== null) clearTimeout(this.nextClickTimeoutId);
    this.nextClickTimeoutId = null;
  }
}
```

The visual click indicator listens for `CustomEvent('geiger:click')` dispatched on each `emitClick()` and triggers a CSS class for the pulse animation.

### Tuning

The `SAMPLE_RATIO` is a perceptual calibration. Test at the canonical slider positions; the goal is "musical, recognisable, not annoying" across the full mass range. Real Geiger counters go louder than this would; the audio bar here is "interesting and informative" rather than "documentary realism." Tune by ear with the panel scrolled into view at 1 g → 100 g → 10 kg and adjust until each position sounds *different* in a recognisable way.

## Glow rendering notes

The `<CubeGlowOverlay />` component from Stage 3 produces the glow params via two-channel scaling (intensity and colour temperature, independent); this stage wires them into the actual rendered cube. Two layers:

1. **Outer atmospheric glow** — positioned div behind the cube with a radial gradient using the interpolated colour, intensity-driven opacity, and intensity-driven `bloomPx`. Centred on the cube, extent ~2× cube edge. Blend mode `screen` so it reads as light, not paint.

2. **Cube surface emission** — `filter: brightness()` and `box-shadow` on the cube sprite itself, using the same colour and intensity. At higher masses the cube reads as actively emitting, not just lit.

Use CSS custom properties `--glow-color`, `--glow-opacity`, `--glow-bloom` so transitions are smooth as the slider moves. Both channels animate independently — colour temperature shifts feel different from brightness shifts.

Test the slider scrubbing across 1 g → 10 kg — the glow should brighten and warm continuously, not flicker between discrete states. Specifically: at 10 g and 100 g and 1 kg, the colour shift should be perceptible *and* the brightness shift should be more dramatic. If both feel like the same animation, something's wrong with the channel separation.
